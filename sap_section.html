Statistical Analysis Plan (Mock Section)

Phase II Adaptive Oncology Trial — Time-to-Progression Endpoint

Author

Cris Taylor

Published

June 9, 2026

Document status. This is a mock SAP section developed for portfolio demonstration purposes. Cross-references such as “the protocol” or “Section 8.1” are illustrative rather than literal. Formal CDISC ADaM dataset and TLF specifications would accompany a real SAP.

1. Study objectives

1.1 Primary objective

To compare the time from randomization to disease progression or death from any cause (progression-free survival, PFS) between patients receiving the experimental therapy and patients receiving standard-of-care control, in patients with the target indication.

1.2 Secondary objectives

  • Compare overall survival between treatment arms.
  • Characterize the safety profile of the experimental therapy.
  • Estimate response rate and duration of response.

2. Primary estimand (ICH E9(R1))

The primary estimand is specified through the five attributes of the ICH E9(R1) framework. A treatment-policy strategy is used for the most common intercurrent event (treatment discontinuation), reflecting an intent-to-treat philosophy appropriate for a Phase II exploratory study.

Attribute

Specification

Treatment

Experimental therapy administered per protocol-defined schedule vs standard-of-care control, both continued until disease progression, unacceptable toxicity, or withdrawal of consent.

Population

Patients with the target indication meeting all inclusion criteria and no exclusion criteria as specified in the protocol.

Endpoint (variable)

Time from date of randomization to date of first documented disease progression per RECIST 1.1 (assessed by investigator) or death from any cause, whichever occurs first.

Intercurrent events

Treatment discontinuation for any reason: treatment-policy strategy (events occurring after discontinuation are included).

Population-level summary

Hazard ratio (experimental vs control) with 95% confidence interval, estimated from a Cox proportional-hazards model.

3. Endpoints

3.1 Primary endpoint

Progression-free survival (PFS), defined as the time from randomization to the earlier of:

  1. first documented disease progression (RECIST 1.1, investigator-assessed); or
  2. death from any cause.

Patients without an event are censored at the last tumor assessment date prior to data cut-off. The censoring rules follow the FDA 2018 guidance on time-to-event endpoints in oncology.

3.2 Key secondary endpoints

  • Overall survival (OS) — time from randomization to death from any cause.
  • Objective response rate (ORR) — proportion of patients achieving complete or partial response per RECIST 1.1.
  • Duration of response (DoR) — for responders only, time from first documented response to disease progression or death.
  • Time to treatment discontinuation (TTD) — time from randomization to permanent discontinuation of experimental therapy.

3.3 Safety endpoints

Frequency of treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAEs) by MedDRA system organ class and preferred term, graded by CTCAE v5.0, summarized descriptively by treatment arm. Treatment-related TEAEs, serious AEs, and TEAEs leading to discontinuation are summarized separately.

4. Statistical hypotheses

4.1 Primary hypothesis

Let \(\lambda_E(t)\) and \(\lambda_C(t)\) denote the instantaneous hazards of PFS at time \(t\) in the experimental and control arms, respectively. The hazard ratio is \(HR = \lambda_E(t) / \lambda_C(t)\), assumed constant under proportional hazards.

  • \(H_0\): \(HR \geq 1\) (no benefit or harm from experimental therapy)
  • \(H_1\): \(HR < 1\) (experimental therapy reduces hazard of PFS event)

The hypothesis is tested at a one-sided significance level of 0.025 using a stratified log-rank test (with the Cox-PH-derived z-statistic compared against the stage 2 O’Brien-Fleming efficacy boundary).

5. Analysis populations

  • Intent-to-treat (ITT): all randomized patients, analyzed in the treatment arm to which they were assigned regardless of treatment received. This is the primary analysis population.
  • Modified ITT (mITT): ITT subset of patients with at least one post-baseline tumor assessment. Used for sensitivity analyses.
  • Safety population: all patients who received at least one dose of study treatment, analyzed by actual treatment received.

6. Sample-size justification

6.1 Phase II screening rationale

This is a Phase II screening trial, not a confirmatory study. The maximum sample size of N = 120 (60 per arm) is deliberately small compared to the n ≈ 791 that rpact’s getSampleSizeSurvival returns for an 80%-powered confirmatory trial at HR = 0.70 under this alpha-spending. The trial is sized for a go/no-go efficiency decision under operational constraints (accrual, budget), not to definitively reject moderate effect sizes. A confirmatory Phase III would scale up.

6.2 Demonstrated operating characteristics

The selected design achieves the following operating characteristics in the simulation report (report/index.qmd):

  • Type I error (one-sided): 0.019 (adaptive) / 0.021 (fixed), both below the 0.025 nominal level.
  • Power across non-null effects (adaptive design):
    • HR = 0.85: 7.5%
    • HR = 0.75: 13.9%
    • HR = 0.65: 23.8%
    • HR = 0.55: 38.7%
  • Probability of early futility stop (adaptive design):
    • Harmful (HR = 1.15): 48%
    • Null (HR = 1.00): 36%
    • Mild benefit (HR = 0.85): 22%
    • Very strong benefit (HR = 0.55): 6%
  • Expected sample-size savings: 5.4% under the null, 7.7% under the harmful direction.

The full simulation (12,000 trials across six HR scenarios) is reported in the main simulation report.

7. Primary analysis method

7.1 Group-sequential test

The primary analysis is a Cox proportional-hazards model with treatment arm as the sole covariate, fit at the planned final analysis (after follow-up completion at month 24 or after all patients have been on study for at least 12 months, whichever is later). The treatment effect is summarized as the hazard ratio (experimental vs control) with 95% confidence interval.

The decision rule applies the O’Brien-Fleming group-sequential boundary from a 2-stage design with one interim look at 30% information time under the design alternative HR = 0.70, at one-sided \(\alpha = 0.025\):

  • Stage 1 (interim): trial may stop for futility (see Section 8) but not for efficacy. No efficacy z-boundary is applied at interim.
  • Stage 2 (final): \(z\)-boundary = 1.969. Reject \(H_0\) if the final Cox-derived z-statistic (\(z = -\hat{\beta} / \mathrm{se}(\hat{\beta})\), where \(\hat{\beta}\) is the log-HR estimate) exceeds the stage 2 boundary AND the HR point estimate is below 1.

The Cox PH proportional hazards assumption is assessed via Schoenfeld residual tests (Section 10).

8. Adaptive design procedures

8.1 Interim analysis

A single interim analysis is conducted when 12 observed events have accumulated — approximately 30% of the expected total events under the design alternative (HR = 0.70). The interim therefore fires on the basis of statistical information, not enrollment progress. The interim analysis is performed by an independent statistician unblinded to treatment assignment, with results reviewed by an Independent Data Monitoring Committee (IDMC).

8.2 Futility stopping rule

At the interim, a Bayesian exponential survival model is fit to the observed data:

\[ T_i \mid \text{arm}_i, \lambda_C, HR \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda_C \cdot HR^{1\{\text{arm}_i = E\}}), \]

with weakly informative priors \(\lambda_C \sim \text{Gamma}(2, 80)\) (centered at the assumed baseline hazard of 0.025/month) and \(\log HR \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\).

The trial stops for futility if the interim posterior probability of clinically meaningful benefit is below threshold:

\[ \Pr(HR < 0.70 \mid \text{interim data}) < 0.20. \]

A futility stop results in \(H_0\) being accepted; no statistical adjustment is applied to the type I error because futility stopping is binding.

8.3 Response-adaptive randomization

If the trial continues past interim, the allocation ratio is updated from 1:1 to a Thompson-style scheme:

\[ \alpha_E = \max\left(0.20,\, \min\left(0.80,\, \sqrt{\Pr(HR < 1 \mid \text{data})}\right)\right), \]

where \(\alpha_E\) is the probability of assigning the next patient to the experimental arm. The 20%–80% caps prevent extreme imbalance that would inflate variance and complicate the final analysis.

Allocation is re-evaluated every 20 enrollees by refitting the Bayesian model on accrued data. The interim posterior and each refit posterior are documented in trial records for regulatory traceability.

8.4 Operating characteristics

Full operating characteristics across six HR scenarios (1.15, 1.00, 0.85, 0.75, 0.65, 0.55) are reported in report/index.qmd §5 and Figures 5.1–5.4. Type I error is controlled at 0.019 under the null hypothesis (adaptive design) / 0.021 (fixed design), verified by 1,000 simulated trials per scenario (MCSE on Type I ≈ 0.004).

9. Handling of missing data and intercurrent events

9.1 Missing data on primary endpoint

The primary endpoint (PFS) is fully observed (either as an event or as a censoring time) by design. No imputation is performed for tumor assessments missed within tolerance windows; censoring uses last documented evaluable assessment.

9.2 Intercurrent events

Per the primary estimand (Section 2):

  • Treatment discontinuation: treatment-policy strategy. Events occurring after discontinuation are included in the analysis.
  • Switching to alternative therapy: treatment-policy strategy; data collection continues per protocol.
  • Death: a component of the primary endpoint (composite endpoint treatment).

9.3 Definition of analysis cut-off

The data cut-off for the primary analysis is the date 24 months after the first patient is randomized (or 12 months after the last patient is randomized, whichever is later). Patients without an event at cut-off are censored at their last tumor assessment date.

10. Sensitivity analyses

10.1 Stratified Cox PH

A stratified Cox model stratifying on baseline factors with potential PH violation (e.g., prior systemic therapy line, performance status) is fit as a sensitivity analysis. Results are reported alongside the primary unstratified analysis.

10.2 Bayesian Weibull AFT

A parametric Weibull accelerated-failure-time model is fit to the same data as a parametric sensitivity analysis. Posterior medians and 95% credible intervals on the hazard ratio (computed as the inverse of the posterior time ratio) are reported. Agreement with the Cox PH estimate provides reassurance that the proportional-hazards assumption is not materially violated. This dual reporting is illustrated for TCGA-BRCA in report/index.qmd Section 6.3.

10.3 Schoenfeld diagnostic

The proportional-hazards assumption is formally tested via survival::cox.zph() using scaled Schoenfeld residuals. Any covariate with \(p < 0.05\) is investigated graphically, and a stratified Cox model is fit as the sensitivity analysis if the global test is violated.

10.4 Alternative estimand: hypothetical strategy

A secondary analysis under the “hypothetical strategy” (assuming treatment discontinuation did not occur) is performed by censoring patients at the time of discontinuation. This analysis supplements but does not replace the primary treatment-policy analysis.

Key findings.

  • Type I error is robust to the threshold. Under the null, rejection rate is 0.013–0.017 across the sweep — well below the 0.025 nominal at every threshold. Futility-only stopping is binding (cannot reject H0), so increasing \(\theta\) cannot inflate Type I.
  • Power is essentially invariant to the threshold. At very-strong benefit (HR = 0.55), reject rate is 39.3%–40.0% across \(\theta\); at strong (HR = 0.65), 21.3%–22.7%. The differences are within MC noise for 300 sims (MC SE ~0.028).
  • Pr(futility stop) scales linearly with \(\theta\) under null and harmful (21% → 45% under null; 31% → 57% under harmful as \(\theta\) goes 0.10 → 0.30) and modestly under benefit (very-strong: 0.7% → 7.7%).
  • E[N] decreases with \(\theta\) as more futility stops shorten trials. Under harmful: 114 → 109. Modest absolute savings, but cumulative ethical benefit across many trials in a program.

Selected threshold (\(\theta = 0.20\)) is defensible. The choice sits near the middle of the sweep and achieves a 48% futility-stop probability under harmful (vs 57% at \(\theta = 0.30\)) without any detectable power cost vs the more conservative \(\theta = 0.10\). A more aggressive value (0.30) is plausible if maximum patient-sparing under no benefit is prioritized over a small probability of stopping under true (mild) benefit.

11. Safety analyses

Treatment-emergent AEs (TEAEs), defined as AEs with onset on or after the first dose of study treatment, are summarized by:

  • System organ class and preferred term per MedDRA (current version at database lock).
  • Maximum severity (CTCAE v5.0).
  • Treatment arm.

Safety analysis is descriptive only; no formal hypothesis tests are performed. Key safety outputs include:

  • Overall TEAE summary (any grade, grade ≥ 3, serious, fatal, treatment-related).
  • TEAE table by SOC and PT, sorted by frequency.
  • AE leading to discontinuation table.
  • Laboratory shift tables (baseline to worst on-treatment grade).

A Safety Review Committee reviews aggregated safety data at the interim analysis in parallel with the IDMC futility review.
